WNBA Rolling Window Evaluation

A plain-English version of what we tested, why we tested it, and what the results mean.

Short answer

We tested whether a team's recent games can help predict whether they will win their next game. We tried looking back over different numbers of games, like the last 3 games, last 5 games, last 10 games, and last 20 games.

So far, very short windows like 3 or 5 games do not look as useful. Looking back around 10 to 20 games has worked better. After adding more basketball context, the best simple lookback is still around 20 games.

The Basic Idea

Imagine asking: "How has this team been playing lately?" To answer that, we average their recent stats.

If a team scored 80, 86, and 74 points in its last 3 games, its 3-game scoring average is:

(80 + 86 + 74) / 3 = 80 points per game

We do that for many stats, not just points. Then we ask whether those recent averages help predict the next game.

What Does "Rolling Window" Mean?

A rolling window is just "the last few games." The number tells us how many games we look back.

Window Plain meaning
n=3 Look at the team's last 3 games.
n=10 Look at the team's last 10 games.
n=20 Look at the team's last 20 games.

We tested 3, 5, 8, 10, 12, 14, 16, 20.

What Goes Into Each Prediction?

Each row is one team in one game. Since every game has two teams, each game creates two rows: one for Team A and one for Team B.

1. Look backward Use only games that happened before the game being predicted.
2. Average recent stats Calculate recent averages for the team and its opponent.
3. Predict win or loss Estimate how likely the team is to win the game.

Stats We Used

The model looks at recent team stats such as:

  • Points scored
  • Points allowed
  • Scoring margin
  • Assists
  • Rebounds
  • Steals
  • Blocks
  • Turnovers
  • Field goal percentage
  • Three-point percentage
  • Free throw percentage
  • Whether the team is home or away
  • How many rest days each team had
  • Whether either team is on a back-to-back
  • Whether one team has more rest than the other
  • How often each team has been winning recently
  • How often each team has been playing close games recently

Extra Basketball Context We Added

We added clues that coaches and fans naturally care about, not just box-score averages.

  • Is the team playing at home?
  • Did one team get more rest?
  • Is either team playing the second game in two days?
  • Has the team been winning recently?
  • Has the team been in a lot of close games recently?
  • Does one team's recent scoring match up well against the opponent's recent defense?

Team vs Opponent

We do not only look at one team by itself. For each stat, we compare the team to its opponent.

For example, for scoring:

  • Team's recent points scored
  • Opponent's recent points scored
  • Difference between the two

This matters because scoring 82 points per game means something different if the opponent has recently been scoring 90 compared with 72.

How the Model Thinks

The model is like a scoreboard for clues. Some clues push the prediction toward "more likely to win." Other clues push it toward "less likely to win."

Helpful clues might be:

  • The team has had a strong scoring margin recently.
  • The team is playing at home.
  • The opponent has been allowing a lot of points.

At the end, the model gives a probability, like "this team has a 58% chance to win."

How We Avoid Cheating

The most important rule: when predicting a game, we only use information that would have been known before that game started.

We do not use the current game's points, rebounds, turnovers, or final score to predict that same game. That would be like reading the answer key before taking the test.

How We Checked If It Worked

We tested it season by season starting in 2023.

  1. To predict 2023 games, train on seasons before 2023.
  2. To predict 2024 games, train on seasons before 2024.
  3. To predict 2025 games, train on seasons before 2025.

This is closer to real life because the model only learns from the past before predicting the future.

What We Found

Window What happened
3 and 5 Too short. These were weaker than the longer windows.
8 Better than the shortest windows, but not the best.
10 to 20 Generally the strongest range.
20 Best single window in the completed-season 2023-2025 check.
Mixed Lets each stat use its own window. It was very close to the best single window and sometimes better.

Which Clues Mattered Most?

We also checked what happened when we scrambled one clue at a time. If scrambling a clue makes the predictions worse, that clue was probably useful.

Clue Plain-English meaning
Home court The biggest factor in the latest check. Being home helped a lot.
Recent scoring margin difference Teams that had been outscoring opponents by more than their opponent had were stronger picks.
Team recent margin A team that had recently been winning by more points looked better.
Opponent recent margin An opponent that had recently been winning by more points made the game harder.
Points allowed difference Recent defense mattered: allowing fewer points than the opponent was a good sign.

What Is "Mixed"?

Instead of forcing every stat to use the same number of games, mixed windows let each stat choose its own lookback length.

For example:

  • Scoring might work best using the last 12 games.
  • Rebounding might work best using the last 20 games.
  • Turnovers might work best using the last 10 or 12 games.

This makes sense because some stats may stabilize quickly, while others may need more games before they tell a reliable story.

Plain-English Conclusion

The main lesson so far is that "very recent form" is not enough by itself. Looking back only 3 or 5 games is probably too noisy. A longer view, roughly 10 to 20 games, has been more useful for predicting wins and losses.

Right now, n=20 is the best simple choice for completed seasons. The biggest useful clues are home court and recent team strength, especially scoring margin.

Next Step

The next useful improvement is to add more basketball context. For example, instead of only asking "how well has this team scored lately?", we can ask "how well has this team scored compared with the quality of defenses it has faced?"
